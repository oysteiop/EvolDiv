Dear Colleagues,
My collaborators, Scott Armbruster, Geir Bolstad, Christophe Pélabon and Thomas Hansen, and I are currently working on a project linking trait evolutionary potential (evolvability) and divergence of plant populations and species. We have worked for some time compiling a large database of trait evolvabilities (mean-scaled genetic variances) in general (see Hansen et al. 2011 Evolutionary Biology), and of plant traits in particular (see Opedal et al. 2017 Evolution, Opedal 2019 New Phytologist).
 
In the current project, we would like to link patterns of within-population genetic/phenotypic variances and covariances to the divergence of populations, thus assessing the role of evolvability in population divergence (see Bolstad et al. 2014 Phil. Trans. B). To this end, we are currently compiling paired data on within-population and among-population variances in plant traits.
 
We are contacting you because either (1) you have published data on genetic variances and/or population divergence in plants, (2) we believe you may have access to such data and, importantly, (3) we think you may be interested in this project. We would like to invite you to collaborate on the project by providing relevant data on systems you are working on. All those providing at least one dataset will be asked to contribute to the writing of the paper, and thus offered co-authorship.
 
Upon publication of the first paper from the project, we intend to make the data used publicly available (e.g. in Dryad), which we see as a great benefit to the community of plant evolutionary biologists. Thus, by providing data, you agree to release the data for this purpose.
 
If you have access to relevant data (published, unpublished or both) and are interested in taking part in this project, we ask that you contact us (oystein.opedal@helsinki.fi) before sending the data, so that we can discuss any details for the particular dataset(s). (Please see below for a summary of data requirements.)
 
We look much forward to hearing from you!

Data requirements
An ideal dataset would comprise a complete additive-genetic variance matrix (G matrix) for a good number of traits (say 5 or more) paired with measurements of a good number of population means (say 5 or more) for the same traits in a similar, common environment (typically a greenhouse). However, we are aware that such datasets are probably very rare, and we are therefore more than happy to consider other data types.
 
Minimum requirements:
  • Within-population variance with summary statistics (means, phenotypic variances, sample sizes, measurement units, measures of uncertainty when available) for at least one population and population means from at least 2 populations for at least 1 trait. It is important that the traits are measured the same way at the within and the among population level.
  • Within-population variances should preferably be additive genetic variances (or narrow-sense heritabilities + phenotypic variances), although broad-sense genetic variances as well as phenotypic variances may also be used.
  • Preferably: When more than one trait is measured, correlations or covariances among traits.
  • Population means should preferably be measured in a common garden (typically greenhouse or similar), although field measurements can also be used.
Useful metadata
  • Self-compatibility status, mating system (categorical, i.e. selfing, mixed-mating, outcrossing), outcrossing rate (specified as species mean or population-specific)
  • Pollinator data, e.g. principal pollinator genera/species, range of pollinator genera/species.
  • FST among populations measured, or other measures of genetic distance.
  • Geographic coordinates of populations, or max distance between study populations.
